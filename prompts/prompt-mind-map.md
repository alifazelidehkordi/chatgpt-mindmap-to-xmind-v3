# Improved Prompt for Generating High-Yield, Concise Mind Maps Optimized for Oral Exams

## Core Task (Reinforced) - Oral Exam Edition

Read and deeply understand the **entire** source text first.

Rebuild it as a **concise, high-yield, concept-based, highly interconnected mind map** optimized for **rapid review and strong performance in oral exams** (ideal full review time: 5-8 minutes).

**The output must NOT be:**
- A rearranged or lightly edited version of the source order
- A long numbered list of topics (1., 2., 3. ... 14.)
- Scattered fragments of the same concept across multiple branches
- Paragraph-like long nodes or textbook-style explanations
- Inclusion of low-yield details, rare variants, or verbose elaborations

**Instead:**
1. Fully digest the whole content.
2. Identify the main overarching topic + **major conceptual clusters/entities**.
3. **Aggressively consolidate** every piece of information belonging to the same conceptual cluster — regardless of where it appeared in the source.
4. Merge duplicates and remove low-value repetition.
5. Reorganize strictly according to **conceptual logic, mechanisms, relationships, comparisons, and exam/high-yield value**.
6. Make logical connections explicit (cause → effect, structure → function, problem → compensation → consequence, A differs from B because...).

## Mandatory High-Yield Filter (Critical New Step - Apply Before Any Structuring)

This is the key upgrade to eliminate "too detailed" maps while keeping **every important high-yield point** for oral exams.

For **every single piece of information** in the source, apply this filter **silently** before deciding what to include:

**Include the information only if it meets at least TWO of the following criteria:**
- It is foundational to understanding the core mechanisms, definitions, or logic of the topic.
- It is highly likely to be tested in an oral exam (classic presentations, diagnostic algorithms, first-line treatments, major complications, key differentials/distinctions).
- It represents a common pitfall, exception, "favorite question" area, or mnemonic-worthy point that examiners frequently probe.
- It can be expressed in a short phrase (ideally ≤ 12 words) that connects clearly to other concepts.

**Aggressively prune / exclude / heavily condense if:**
- It is a long explanation, detailed mechanism not asked at oral level, or textbook filler.
- It is a rare variant, low-prevalence fact, or minor detail that doesn't change management or understanding significantly.
- It is repetitive or adds little new value beyond what is already covered more concisely elsewhere.
- It would make nodes long or branches cluttered without improving exam readiness.

**Goal of filter:** The final map contains *all* the important high-yield content, presented in the most compact, interconnected, and exam-ready form possible. Nothing important is lost, but everything low-yield or overly verbose is removed.

## Critical Improvement: Mandatory Concept Consolidation (Works for Any Topic)

This is the core upgrade. The previous version still allowed fragmentation in complex texts.

### Mandatory Pre-Processing Workflow (do this silently before writing OPML):

**Step 1: Identify Major Conceptual Clusters**
After reading the full text and applying the High-Yield Filter, ask:
- What are the big, relatively self-contained ideas, entities, processes, or themes that deserve their own top-level branch?
- These clusters should group related high-yield information that is currently scattered.
(Examples depending on topic type:
- **Medical / Clinical topics**: Diagnostic framework, major diseases as unified clusters, cross-cutting comparisons
- **Historical / Event-based topics**: Major periods/events + causes + effects + consequences + key actors
- **Biological / Pathway topics**: Key processes + regulatory points + downstream effects + outcomes
- **Conceptual / Theoretical topics**: Core concept + mechanisms + counterpoints + implications
- **Process or Mechanism-heavy topics**: Stepwise sequence + decision points + feedback loops + variations + exam relevance)

**Step 2: Total Consolidation Rule (Non-Negotiable)**
- Once you decide a cluster deserves its own top-level branch (e.g. "Graves Disease" or "Photosynthesis Light Reactions"), **collect EVERY relevant high-yield piece of information** about it from the entire source (that passed the filter) and put it inside that single branch.
- Do **not** leave important sub-information in other numbered sections or scattered branches.
- If something overlaps multiple clusters, put the detailed version in the primary cluster and add concise distinctions or cross-references in the dedicated "Comparisons & Relationships" section.

**Step 3: Give Each Major Cluster a Logical Internal Structure**
Do **not** use a rigid template for all topics. Choose the internal organization that best fits the nature of that cluster.

**Critical Rule:** Every major cluster **must conclude with** a dedicated `🎯 Oral Exam High-Yield` sub-branch (see updated patterns below). This subsection should contain likely oral questions, common traps/pitfalls, mnemonics, short model answer phrases, and key distinctions.

Common useful patterns (adapt freely, keep nodes short, limit direct children per section to ~5-7 where reasonable):

**Pattern A – For Clinical/Medical Entities (Optimized for Oral Exams)**
```
Entity/Disease/Condition Name
├── Core Definition & Epidemiology (1-2 highest-yield facts only)
├── Pathophysiology & Core Mechanism (concise cause-effect)
├── Clinical Features (top 4-6 most characteristic or frequently tested only)
├── Diagnosis (key lab/imaging patterns + high-yield test logic & interpretation)
├── Treatment & Management (first-line + critical considerations & monitoring)
├── Complications, Prognosis & Red Flags
└── 🎯 Oral Exam High-Yield: Likely Questions, Common Traps, Mnemonics, Model Answer Phrases, Key Distinctions from Differentials
```

**Pattern B – For Processes, Mechanisms or Pathways**
```
Process/Mechanism Name
├── Trigger / Starting Point
├── Main Steps (sequential with → arrows, high-yield only)
├── Key Molecules, Energy/Information Flow & Regulatory Points
├── Connection to Downstream Processes / Overall Functional Outcome
├── Important Variations, Exceptions or Control Points
└── 🎯 Oral Exam High-Yield: Common Questions, Traps, Mnemonics, Model Answer Phrases
```

**Pattern C – For Conceptual or Argument-Based Topics**
```
Core Concept: X
├── Precise Definition (high-yield phrasing)
├── Supporting Mechanisms / Key Evidence (concise)
├── Limitations, Counterpoints or Conditions Where It Doesn't Apply
├── Interactions with Related Concepts
├── Practical / Clinical / Real-World Implications
└── 🎯 Oral Exam High-Yield: Common Questions, Traps, Mnemonics, Model Answer Phrases
```

**Pattern D – For Historical or Event Topics**
```
Major Event / Period
├── Background & Precipitating High-Yield Causes
├── Key Developments / Turning Points (stepwise, concise)
├── Short-term Effects (most important only)
├── Long-term Consequences & Legacy (exam-relevant)
├── Important Comparisons (with similar events)
└── 🎯 Oral Exam High-Yield: Common Questions, Traps, Mnemonics, Model Answer Phrases
```

You can mix patterns or create hybrid structures when it makes conceptual sense. The goal is always **clarity + logical flow + easy navigation + instant oral exam readiness** in XMind.

## Exam / Review-Oriented Compression Rules

- Ruthlessly concise thanks to the High-Yield Filter. The mind map should support fast review (ideally completable in 5–8 minutes) and be directly usable for last-minute oral exam prep.
- Nodes should be short phrases or single clear sentences (maximum 1–2 lines, target ≤12 words). Use compact relationship language inside nodes:
  - `A → B because mechanism X`
  - `Graves vs Toxic MNG: diffuse high uptake vs patchy/focal`
  - `If TSH low + uptake low → think leak or exogenous (not true hyperfunction)`
- Prioritize: mechanisms, cause-effect chains, diagnostic/clinical clues, comparisons, distinctions, common traps, mnemonics, must-know exceptions, first-line actions.
- Avoid: long explanations, repeated examples, low-yield facts, source-order preservation without conceptual reason, minor details that did not strongly pass the High-Yield Filter.
- In any list (features, steps, causes, etc.): include only the top 4-6 highest-yield items. If more candidates exist, either group them logically or confirm they passed the filter with high priority.

## Accuracy & Terminology Rules
- Preserve scientific, historical, or domain accuracy.
- Keep standard terminology of the field.
- For bilingual sources (e.g. Persian medical notes), retain key English terms when they are high-yield for exams or international understanding.
- Do not add unsupported details.

## XMind Compatibility Rules
- Valid OPML 2.0
- Clean nested structure
- Short nodes for better visual layout
- Proper XML escaping
- Use 0–2 emojis per major section only for quick visual scanning (avoid overuse)

## Final Validation Checklist (Mandatory before outputting OPML)
- [ ] Did I identify the major conceptual clusters first?
- [ ] Did I apply the **High-Yield Filter** to every piece of information and aggressively prune anything low-yield or verbose?
- [ ] Did I consolidate **all** high-yield information about each cluster into **one** dedicated top-level branch (no important fragments left elsewhere)?
- [ ] Did I avoid creating a long numbered list that follows source order?
- [ ] Is the internal structure of each major branch logical, concise, and helpful for that specific type of content?
- [ ] Does **every major conceptual cluster end with a populated `🎯 Oral Exam High-Yield` subsection** with likely questions, traps, mnemonics, and model answer phrases?
- [ ] Are comparisons and logical connections explicit and easy to find?
- [ ] Is the overall map concise enough to review in 5-8 minutes while still containing every important high-yield point needed for oral exams?
- [ ] Would someone reviewing this in XMind quickly understand the big picture and key relationships **and feel well-prepared to handle oral exam questions** on the topic?
- [ ] Are all nodes short, scannable, and free of unnecessary detail?

If any item fails, go back and apply the High-Yield Filter more strictly or consolidate/reorganize further.
